Chapter 1 · The pitch in one paragraph

Same loop. Different product.

Pump.fun turned token launches into a game show: anybody can spin up a coin for a few bucks, the market pumps the ones it likes, and the winners "graduate" to a bigger exchange. bMovies runs the same game loop — but instead of minting another dog-themed shitcoin, you mint a film. Every launch is a real production with a treatment, a storyboard, a poster, and a director. Every royalty share is a real on-chain asset that pays out when someone presses play.

The degenerate energy is a feature, not a bug. We're pointing a firehose of speculative attention at film financing, which has needed it for a hundred years.

Chapter 5 · Why this hook works

The cultural shortcut. Three words, everyone nods.

"It's Pump.fun for movies" is a cheat code. In one sentence your audience already knows: anyone can launch, the market picks the winners, the mechanism is a bonding curve of attention, and graduation is the promise. You don't have to explain agents, royalty shares, or BSV. You just have to say the phrase, point at the ladder, and let them click.

The trick is leaning into it without becoming it. Pump.fun launches a joke; bMovies launches a film. Pump.fun graduates to Raydium; bMovies graduates to a premiere. The comparison sells the front door. The back door is actually a film studio.

Honesty note

Royalty shares, not meme coins. The things you buy on bMovies are fractional claims on a film's revenue. They pay out when someone pays to watch. They're not utility tokens, not coin offerings, and not "the next 100x". If nobody watches the film, they earn zero. We've written this one the way we wrote Ponzinomics — with the odds visible and the house edge stated.

The degen hook is a marketing skin. The engine underneath is the same one the rest of the site sits on: a studio marketplace where agents produce real artifacts on a 28-step pipeline, payments settle on BSV mainnet, and the creator keeps 99% of the shares.
